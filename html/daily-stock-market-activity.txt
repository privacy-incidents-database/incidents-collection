Business Day 
Wall St. Is Lower on Energy Sector Drop

By THE ASSOCIATED PRESSFEB. 1, 2016


Inside

United States markets were lower on Monday as the price of oil took another tumble in the face of renewed concerns about the Chinese and American economies.KEEPING SCORE In early afternoon trading, the Dow Jones industrial average fell 0.48 percent, and the Standard & Poor’s 500-stock index was down 0.41 percent. The Nasdaq composite index dropped 0.25 percent.OIL PROBLEMS Crude oil prices moved lower again after posting solid gains last week. United States crude fell $2.12, to $31.50 a barrel. Natural gas took an even steeper tumble, falling more than 7 percent.Energy stocks fell in tandem with oil and gas prices, and the energy component of the S.&P.; 500 lost 2.5 percent. Southwestern Energy shares declined 7.4 percent, Transocean shares dropped 6.8 percent and Chesapeake Energy stock dropped 8.2 percent.FACTORY WORRIES The economic data out of the United States did not help investor sentiment either. A survey of manufacturing activity came in at 48.2, below expectations.RECOVERING FROM ILLNESS Chipotle Mexican Grill shares rose 5 percent after news reports said federal regulators planned to declare the E. coli outbreak at the restaurant chain to be over.TWITTER CHATTER Twitter shares rose 8 percent after reports that the company was in talks with private equity firms about a possible acquisition or investment.HEALTH CARE MERGER Shares of Alere jumped 45 percent after Abbott Laboratories announced it was purchasing Alere, a health care company focused on diagnostics, for $5.8 billion. Abbot shares fell 0.3 percent.EUROPEAN MARKETS In Germany, the DAX fell 0.4 percent, and in France, the CAC 40 lost 0.5 percent. The FTSE 100 was down 0.4 percent in Britain.CHINA MANUFACTURING An index based on a survey of factory purchasing managers fell to 49.4 in January, its lowest in more than three years, in the latest sign of weakness for the world’s No. 2 economy behind that of the United States. January’s reading was down from December’s 49.7; numbers below 50 indicate contraction.THE QUOTE “There are precious few indicators that point to a recovery within China, and this continues to spell bad news for the global economy, which has been hugely reliant upon Chinese demand to help compensate for weak Western demand post-2007,” said Joshua Mahony, a market analyst at IG.ASIA’S DAY The Nikkei 225 rose 2 percent in Japan, while in Hong Kong, the Hang Seng fell 0.5 percent. The Shanghai composite index lost 1.8 percent in China. The Kospi was up 0.7 percent in South Korea. In Australia, the S.&P.;/ASX 200 gained 0.8 percent.CURRENCIES AND BONDS The dollar fell to 120.92 yen from 121.10 yen on Friday. The euro strengthened to $1.0897 from $1.0829. Government bond prices fell. The yield on the 10-year Treasury note rose to 1.95 percent.
